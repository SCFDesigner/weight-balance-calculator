Aerodynamic Effects of Engine Failure

Pitch Down (Lateral Axis): Loss of accelerated slipstream over the horizontal stabilizer causes it to produce less negative lift → aircraft pitches down. Compensate: increase back pressure on yoke.

Roll Toward Dead Engine (Longitudinal Axis): Loss of accelerated slipstream over the dead engine's wing causes loss of lift → roll toward dead engine. Compensate: bank slightly into operating engine.

Yaw Toward Dead Engine (Vertical Axis): Asymmetric thrust causes yaw toward dead engine. Windmilling prop creates significant drag. Compensate: rudder toward operating engine.

The Critical Engine (PAST)

The engine whose failure most adversely affects performance and handling. In most twins, the left engine is critical.

  • P-Factor: Asymmetric thrust at high AOA. Descending blade (right side) produces more thrust. Left engine's descending blade thrust arm is farther from centerline → more adverse yaw when lost.
  • Accelerated Slipstream: Prop slipstream increases lift over wing. Loss of slipstream on dead engine side → roll toward dead engine. Also reduces horizontal stabilizer effectiveness → pitch down.
  • Spiraling Slipstream: Corkscrew airflow striking vertical stabilizer creates yaw tendency. Loss of spiraling slipstream decreases rudder effectiveness.
  • Torque: Aircraft tends to rotate opposite propeller rotation. With one engine out, torque from operating engine creates additional rolling moment.

Asymmetrical Thrust

Unequal thrust from engines creates yaw toward dead engine. Dead engine's windmilling prop adds significant drag, increasing yaw. Rudder toward operating engine counteracts yaw. More rudder needed as airspeed decreases — at Vmc, full rudder may be required.

Zero Sideslip

Sideslip (undesirable): Wings level + rudder to hold heading → sideward lift from vertical stabilizer → aircraft sideslips → increased drag → vicious cycle requiring more rudder.

Zero sideslip (desirable):

  • Apply rudder toward operating engine (dead foot, dead engine)
  • Bank 2–5° into operating engine
  • Lift vector horizontal component counteracts yaw → less rudder needed → less drag
  • Ball should be deflected ½ ball width toward operating engine

Vmc — Minimum Controllable Airspeed

Manufacturer-determined speed below which directional control cannot be maintained with critical engine inoperative and operating engine at takeoff power.

Certification requirements: Maintain heading within 20°; after recovery, maintain straight flight with ≤5° bank.

Vmc Factors (CRMALOFT):

  • C — Critical engine windmilling (more drag → higher Vmc)
  • R — Rearward CG (less rudder leverage → higher Vmc)
  • M — Max takeoff power (most asymmetric thrust → higher Vmc)
  • A — Airborne, out of ground effect (more induced drag → higher Vmc)
  • L — Landing gear up (less keel effect → higher Vmc)
  • O — Operating engine at takeoff/max power (greatest asymmetric thrust → higher Vmc)
  • F — Flaps in takeoff position (increased yawing moment → higher Vmc)
  • T — Trim set for takeoff (reduced control forces → higher Vmc)

Vmc decreases with altitude because the operating engine produces less power. Published as IAS; TAS at Vmc increases with altitude.

Vmc Certification — CRM ALOFT (Detailed)

Each factor below explains how it affects Vmc and single-engine performance. All conditions are set to produce the highest Vmc for certification purposes.

C — Critical Engine Inoperative & Windmilling

↑ Vmc · ↓ Performance

A windmilling prop creates more drag than a feathered one, increasing the yawing moment toward the dead engine. More rudder force is needed to counteract that yaw, so the minimum controllable speed goes up.

Feathered = less drag = less yaw = lower Vmc. Windmilling = max drag = max yaw = higher Vmc. Additionally, windmilling drag reduces total available thrust, degrading performance significantly compared to a feathered prop.

R — Rearward Center of Gravity

↑ Vmc · ↑ Performance

CG position changes the moment arm to the rudder. Forward CG = longer arm = more rudder leverage = lower Vmc. Rearward CG = shorter arm = less rudder leverage = higher Vmc (Law of the Lever).

Performance improves with aft CG because the horizontal stabilizer produces less negative lift (less down force needed to balance), reducing induced drag.

M — Most Unfavorable Takeoff Weight

↑ Vmc · ↑ Performance

A lighter airplane is the most unfavorable weight for Vmc. Two reasons:

  • A heavier airplane has a higher horizontal component of lift at a given bank angle (5°), which helps turn toward the operating engine — more turning force = better control.
  • A heavier airplane resists yawing and rolling toward the dead engine because the operating engine's force is less effective displacing the greater mass (like pushing a heavy door vs. a light one).

A lighter airplane = less resistance to yaw = higher Vmc. However, the lighter airplane has less induced drag and therefore better overall performance.

A — Airborne, Out of Ground Effect

↑ Vmc · ↓ Performance

Light twins are not required to determine Vmc on the ground. Out of ground effect, induced drag is higher → higher AOA required → P-factor and accelerated slipstream effects are more pronounced → greater rudder force needed → Vmc increases.

In ground effect, the downwash deflection reduces induced drag significantly, meaning the airplane performs much better and requires less rudder authority. Out of ground effect, total drag is higher and performance degrades.

L — Landing Gear Retracted

↑ Vmc · ↑ Performance

Extended gear provides three stabilizing effects that lower Vmc:

  1. Keel Effect: Gear lowers the CG below the center of lift, increasing stability — like the keel of a boat holding it upright.
  2. Drag behind CG: Main gear drag acts behind the CG, reducing the effectiveness of the yawing moment from the dead engine — like a child dragging their feet on a bike for stability.
  3. Weathervane Effect: Wheels and doors align themselves with the relative wind, resisting the yaw toward the dead engine — like a weathervane pointing into wind.

Gear retracted removes all three stabilizing effects → more rudder required → higher Vmc. But retracted gear also means less total drag → better performance.

O — Operating Engine at Takeoff/Max Power

↑ Vmc · ↑ Performance

Full power on the operating engine creates the greatest asymmetric thrust → maximum yawing and rolling moment toward the dead engine. If the operating engine were at idle, there would be no yaw and no rudder needed.

For a normally aspirated engine, full power occurs at sea level with standard atmosphere. More power = more asymmetric force = higher Vmc. Obviously, more power also means better performance and slower descent rate OEI.

F — Flaps in Takeoff Position

↑ Vmc · ↑ Performance

Flap settings affect lift and drag differently:

  • Low settings (5°–15°): Significant lift increase, small drag penalty
  • Middle settings (15°–25°): Equal increase in lift and drag
  • High settings (25°–40°): Higher drag penalty than lift developed

At low/takeoff flap settings, the accelerated slipstream effect is more pronounced (less drag behind CG to stabilize). At higher settings, drag aft of the CG creates a stabilizing effect similar to extended gear. More flaps = easier to control = lower Vmc. Takeoff flaps (low or nil) = harder to control = higher Vmc.

With little or no flaps, less drag means more available power → better performance.

T — Trimmed for Takeoff (Rudder)

↑ Vmc · ↑ Performance

Three possible rudder trim configurations:

  1. Trimmed toward operating engine (less foot pressure): Trim tab helps hold rudder in place but decreases total rudder effectiveness (lowers the rudder AOA). Less effective rudder → higher Vmc.
  2. Trim neutral: Tab acts as a rudder extension. Higher foot pressure required, but rudder AOA is higher → more effective → lower Vmc.
  3. Trimmed toward inoperative engine (most foot pressure): Dramatically increases rudder AOA → most effective. However, rudder pressure increases significantly. Certification limit: no more than 150 lbs of force.

Takeoff trim (toward operating engine or neutral) limits rudder effectiveness, producing a higher Vmc. Less trim drag also means better performance.

Single Engine Performance

Loss of one engine = 50% power loss but ≥80% performance loss because remaining engine must overcome drag from failed engine.

Best SE performance requires: Max power on operating engine, minimum drag (gear up, flaps up, feather prop), zero sideslip.

Flaps — General Effects

  • Low settings (5°–15°): significant lift increase, slight drag increase
  • High settings (15°–30°): significant drag increase
  • Increase coefficient of lift, wing camber, nose-down pitch tendency
  • Reduce stall speed at low settings; increase descent angle without increasing airspeed
  • Increase lift/drag ratio at low settings; decrease at high settings

Electrical System

  • Primary power: Two engine-driven generators (40A, 14.2–14.8 VDC each), operating in parallel with integrated voltage regulators and automatic overvoltage protection
  • Secondary power: Main battery 12V, 23Ah + Secondary battery 12V, 13Ah (both lead-acid, fuselage tail left side). EMERG BATT switch puts both in parallel.
  • 5 Buses: Battery Bus (3-source: battery + both generators), LH Generator Bus, RH Generator Bus, LH Avionics Bus, RH Avionics Bus
  • Cross-bus switches: Allow parallel connection of generator/battery buses; OFF isolates generator bus from battery bus and opposite generator
  • FIELD switches: Control generator excitation — OFF puts that generator off-line
  • Avionics switches: Can cut power to respective avionic bus via relay
  • Battery emergency power: 30 minutes
  • System is DC; Voltmeter/ammeter on RH instrument panel; ammeter selector for LH or RH generator
  • External DC power feeds Battery Bus regardless of master switch position (starters can engage with master off)
  • Each generator alone can maintain flight safety if the other fails

Warning, Caution & Advisory Lights

  • RED (Warning): LH/RH OVERVOLT, MAIN/REAR DOOR OPEN, LH/RH LOW COOLANT, LH/RH ENGINE FIRE, LG TRANSITION
  • AMBER (Caution): LH/RH GENERATOR failure, EXT POWER connected, PITOT HEAT failure, GEAR PUMP ON
  • GREEN (Advisory): LH/RH FUEL PUMP ON, PITOT HEAT ON, 3× LG Down & Locked

Engines

  • Type: Bombardier-Rotax 912 S3 — 4-stroke, 4-cylinder horizontally opposed, 1352 cc displacement
  • Single central camshaft, pushrods, OHV
  • Cooling: Cylinder heads liquid-cooled, cylinders ram-air cooled
  • Dry-sump forced lubrication system
  • Dual breakerless CDI ignition; firing order: 1-4-2-3
  • Two constant-depression carburetors per engine
  • Reduction gearbox (2.27:1) with shock absorber
  • Max power: 73.5 kW (98.6 HP) @ 5800 RPM / 2388 prop RPM (5 min limit)
  • Max continuous: 69.0 kW (92.5 HP) @ 5500 RPM / 2265 prop RPM

Cooling System

  • Closed liquid circuit for cylinder heads; ram-air cooling for cylinders
  • Flow: Radiator → water pump (camshaft-driven) → cylinder heads → expansion tank → radiator
  • Expansion tank: Located on top of engine (radiator is below engine level) — accommodates thermal expansion
  • Pressure cap: Fitted with pressure relief valve + return valve on the expansion tank
  • Overflow: Relief valve opens when hot → coolant flows to transparent overflow bottle at atmospheric pressure. On cooling, vacuum sucks coolant back into circuit.
  • Coolant temp sensors at cylinder heads 2 and 3 (hottest)
  • CT max: 120°C (248°F) · CHT max: 135°C (275°F)
  • LOW COOLANT warning: Red annunciator light when level drops below limit

Oil System

  • Dry sump forced lubrication; camshaft-driven oil pump with integrated pressure regulator
  • Flow: Oil tank → oil cooler → oil filter → lubrication points → crankcase → oil tank (via piston blow-by)
  • Thermostatic valve: Regulates oil flow to heat exchanger (oil radiator) based on temperature — allows cold-weather starting
  • Oil tank behind firewall, protected from heat sources; dipstick for level check
  • Oil relief hose discharges to safe zone in cowling away from exhausts
  • Oil pressure: Min 0.8 bar (11.5 psi) below 1400 prop RPM · Normal 2–5 bar (29–72.5 psi) · Max 7 bar (102 psi) — cold start up to 7 bar permissible briefly
  • Oil temp: Min 50°C · Normal 90–110°C (194–230°F) · Max 130°C (266°F)

Propeller & Governor

  • Propeller: MT MTV-21-A-C-F/CF178-05 — 2 wood/composite blades, aluminium hub, diameter 1780 mm (no reduction allowed)
  • Type: Variable pitch, hydraulically controlled, fully featherable
  • Governor: MT P-875-12 — hydraulic; oil pressure reduces pitch
  • If hub loses oil pressure → blades move to high-pitch/low-RPM (feather direction)
  • Power change: Increase: Prop FIRST, then MAP. Decrease: MAP FIRST, then Prop
  • Governor check: slowly and gently 3 purge cycles, 400–500 RPM drop each; push lever if >500 RPM drop. Avoid excessive lever pulling (gearbox wear + detonation risk)
  • RPM: Green 580–2265, Yellow 2265–2388, Red line 2388

Fuel System

  • Two integrated wing tanks: 100L (26.42 gal) each — 200L (52.8 gal) total
  • Usable: 194.4L (51.35 gal) — 97.2L (25.68 gal) per wing
  • Unusable: 5.6L (1.48 gal) — 2.8L (0.74 gal) per wing
  • 4 drain points: 1 gascolator + 1 wing sump per side
  • No fuel transfer — cross-feed only (supply engine from opposite tank)
  • Each selector: Left, Right, Off (Off requires pull + rotate to prevent accidental shutoff)
  • Electric backup fuel pump for engine-driven pump failure
  • Gascolator (sediment-filter bowl) below engine nacelle at lowest point of fuel system
  • Fuel pressure: Min 2.2 psi (0.15 bar) · Max 5.8 psi (0.40 bar)
  • Approved fuels: MOGAS ASTM D4814, MOGAS EN 228 (min RON 95), AVGAS 100LL
  • 100LL may increase valve seat wear and combustion deposits (MOGAS preferred for longevity)

Fuel Delivery (Engine Side)

  • Flow: Tank → coarse filter → fire cock → fine filter → mechanical fuel pump → fuel manifold → two carburetors
  • A return line routes surplus fuel back to the tank and suction side to prevent vapour lock
  • Mechanical fuel pump is engine-driven; electric backup pump available
  • Carb heat controlled via Bowden cable levers on central pedestal (fully pulled = max hot air; forward = OFF)

Landing Gear

  • 3 wheels; electro-hydraulic retractable; 6 couples of micro-switches (2 per leg) for position detection
  • Normal: Reversible electric hydraulic pump; extension aided by gravity
  • Emergency: Pressurized accumulator (min 20 bar) forces gear down via shuttle valves (NLG + MLG)
  • Inadvertent retraction protection: knob must be pulled before pushing UP
  • Accumulator gauge: left side of tail cone; red push-button to recharge if low
  • Emergency extension: two distributors on cabin floor under removable cover near pilot seat
  • Set gear knob to DOWN before emergency extension so indication lights work normally
  • Indications: 3 green (down-locked), 1 red (transit), amber "GEAR PUMP ON" caution
  • Warning horn: gear UP + throttle idle or flaps LAND
  • After emergency extension, apply AMM restoration procedure

Brake System

  • Independent hydraulic brake for each main wheel — no nose wheel brakes
  • Master cylinders (MC-4) on each pilot and copilot rudder pedal
  • Single vented oil reservoir (R-100) feeds pilot-side cylinders, connected to copilot-side
  • Parking brake: PV-1 valve on cabin floor, operated by knob on central pedestal — traps pressure in lines
  • Brakes operable from both pilot and copilot pedals
  • Do NOT press opposite toe brake while steering until pedals realign — may damage mechanism

Flight Controls & Trim

  • Primary controls: ailerons, rudder, stabilator — manual via cables/pulleys/rods
  • No reversible flight controls, no aileron-rudder interconnect
  • Has: rudder trim, aileron trim (ground-adjustable tab), and elevator trim (control wheel between seats)
  • Stabilator with anti-tab winglet (also functions as trim tab)
  • Flaps: single electric actuator, continuous mode; markings: 0°, T/O, FULL

Ignition System

  • Dual, breakerless capacitor discharge ignition (CDI) with integrated generator
  • Two independent charging coils supply the ignition circuits
  • Energy stored in capacitors; external trigger coils actuate discharge to ignition coils
  • Independent from the main electrical generation/distribution system — switches control only engine electrical circuit
  • Firing order: 1-4-2-3
  • WARNING: If ignition switches are ON, propeller movement can cause engine starting — hazard to nearby people

Door Locking System

  • Main door (forward, left): Electro-mechanical lock engages when left engine running (via oil pressure switch)
  • Emergency exit (aft, right): Same system, triggered by right engine oil pressure
  • Manual override lever accessible from inside and outside door panel
  • Post-shutdown pressure drop may delay opening — use override if needed
  • Ditching exit (roof): Manual handle with safety wire; two cables keep door connected when open
  • Warning lights: MAIN DOOR OPEN and REAR DOOR OPEN on annunciator

Pedestal Controls

  • Throttle levers: LH and RH (with friction knob)
  • Propeller pitch levers: LH and RH
  • Carb heat levers: Between throttle and prop levers — fully pulled = max hot air, forward = OFF
  • Choke controls: With friction knob
  • Parking brake knob
  • Windshield defrost knob

Preflight & Towing

  • Tow using the nose gear tow bar
  • Preflight gear check: inspect for damage, proper extension, and brake condition
  • 4 drain points: 1 gascolator + 1 wing sump per side
  • Check emergency gear accumulator pressure gauge (left side of tail cone) — min 20 bar
  • Oil level check: switch off ignitions, turn prop by hand several times to pump oil to tank, then check dipstick

Normal Takeoff

Use manufacturer's Vr or VLOF. If not published: minimum of Vmc or 1.05 × Vs0, whichever is greater.

P2006T: Rotate at 65 KIAS. Short field: use takeoff flaps, rotate at 65, climb at Vx (72 KIAS).

Critical speed on takeoff is Vmc — below it, directional control may be lost.

Engine Failure After Takeoff

Immediate: Maintain control, pitch for VYSE (84 KIAS)

Procedure:

  1. Maintain directional control
  2. Power up (max on operating engine)
  3. Clean up (gear up, flaps up)
  4. Identify (dead foot = dead engine)
  5. Verify (retard throttle of suspected engine)
  6. Feather the failed engine
  7. Secure (mixture, prop, throttle, fuel, ignition)
  8. Checklist

Engine Failure In Flight

  1. Maintain control
  2. Pitch for VYSE
  3. Identify
  4. Verify
  5. Feather
  6. Secure
  7. Troubleshoot if time permits

Engine Restart

  1. Fuel selector ON
  2. Mixture RICH
  3. Prop FULL FORWARD
  4. Throttle CRACKED
  5. Starter ENGAGE

Vmc Demonstration

Setup: Safe altitude, takeoff config (gear up, flaps takeoff), takeoff power both engines.

Procedure: Reduce power on critical engine, maintain heading with rudder + bank into operating engine, slowly reduce airspeed.

Recovery: At first sign of loss of directional control (stall warning, rudder pedal to stop) → reduce power on operating engine → decrease AOA → recover to safe airspeed → reestablish normal flight.

Fuel System

Two integrated wing tanks (100L / 26.42 gal each). Usable: 194.4L (51.35 gal). Cross-feed only, no transfer. Each selector: Left, Right, Off (pull + rotate for Off). Gascolator at lowest point. Electric backup fuel pump.

Landing Gear System (Hydraulic)

Electro-hydraulic retractable gear. Reversible pump for normal ops. Emergency: pressurized accumulator (min 20 bar) forces gear down through shuttle valves. Indications: 3 green, 1 red, amber GEAR PUMP ON.

Brake System

Independent hydraulic brakes for each main wheel. Master cylinders on pilot and copilot pedals. Single vented reservoir. PV-1 parking valve on cabin floor.

Liquid Cooling System

Closed liquid circuit for cylinder heads; ram-air for cylinders. Flow: Radiator → water pump → cylinder heads → expansion tank → radiator. CT max 120°C, CHT max 135°C.

Powerplant

The Tecnam P2006T is equipped with two Rotax 912S3 engines, each producing 73.5 kW (98.6 hp). Both engines rotate clockwise. Four-cylinder, four-stroke, horizontally opposed, 1352 cc displacement. Partially liquid cooled. Each engine uses two carburetors and an integrated reduction gearbox (2.27:1) with shock absorber.

Engine Features

  • Maximum power (5 min): 73.5 kW (98.6 hp) at 5800 RPM / 2388 prop RPM
  • Continuous power: 69.0 kW (92.5 hp) at 5500 RPM / 2265 prop RPM
  • Ignition: Dual breakerless CDI, independent from electrical system
  • Firing order: 1-4-2-3

Propeller System

  • Model: MT MTV-21-A-C-F/CF178-05 — 2 wood/composite blades, aluminium hub
  • Diameter: 1780 mm (no reduction allowed)
  • Type: Variable-pitch, hydraulically controlled, fully featherable
  • Governor: MT P-875-12 — oil pressure reduces pitch; loss of pressure → feather
  • Power change: Increase: Prop FIRST, then MAP. Decrease: MAP FIRST, then Prop
  • Governor check: 3 purge cycles, 400–500 RPM drop each
